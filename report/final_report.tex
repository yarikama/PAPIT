\begin{abstract}
We present \textbf{PAPIT-Distill}, a $1.3$M-parameter MLP that distils
LLaVA-1.5-7B's mid-layer attention into a single-pass image-token
pruner. On TextVQA at $k{=}25\%$ retention (a $10\times$
attention-FLOP reduction) it gains $+12$ points over random pruning
($36.0$ vs.\ $23.7$), essentially recovering the unpruned baseline
($36.7$). On GQA and VQA~v2 at $k{=}50\%$ it closes the gap to
unpruned to within a single point. Scoring a $576$-patch image takes
$0.43$\,ms --- ${\sim}150{\times}$ faster than its CLIP-GradCAM
analogue and ${\sim}500{\times}$ faster than the two-pass oracle it
learns from. En route we report a quantitative \emph{negative result}
that motivates the method: cross-dataset Spearman $\rho{\approx}0$
between CLIP-derived saliency and LLaVA's own LLM attention, ruling
out training-free CLIP-aligned scorers as a class.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

LLaVA-1.5~\cite{liu2024llava15} composes a CLIP ViT-L/14 vision
encoder~\cite{radford2021clip,dosovitskiy2021vit} with a 7B-parameter
language model. Each of the $24{\times}24=576$ image tokens participates
in every self-attention layer~\cite{vaswani2017attention}, making
inference cost quadratic in the number of visual tokens. For a typical
VQA query fewer than half of these tokens are relevant.

A common intuition is that the user's prompt should be enough to choose
which tokens to keep, \emph{without} any additional training. We make
this claim concrete: \emph{can a training-free saliency signal, read
from LLaVA's own CLIP-initialised vision tower, predict which patches
the LLM relies on, well enough to beat a query-agnostic random
baseline?}

We answer this in three stages. First (\S\ref{sec:method}) we instantiate
the idea as \textbf{PAPIT}, a pipeline that inserts a GradCAM-based
top-$k$ between the ViT and the MLP projector. Second
(\S\ref{sec:diagnostic}) we show that PAPIT only \emph{matches} random
pruning, and that a direct alignment diagnostic explains why: the
CLIP-aligned scoring signal has $\rho{\approx}0$ with the LLM's own
attention. Third (\S\ref{sec:distill}) we run a multi-layer
LLM-attention oracle as a positive control --- mid-layer attention
\emph{does} beat random --- and distil that signal into a $1.3$M-param
MLP that runs in single-pass, training-free deployment.

\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\textbf{(i)} A clean training-free token-pruning pipeline for LLaVA-1.5
with $41$--$90\%$ attention-FLOP reduction at $k\!\in\![25\%,75\%]$.
\textbf{(ii)} A cross-dataset \emph{negative result}: CLIP-derived patch
saliency is statistically uncorrelated with LLaVA's LLM attention,
ruling out an entire family of training-free prompt-aware methods.
\textbf{(iii)} \textbf{PAPIT-Distill}, a small MLP distilled from
mid-layer LLM attention that turns the negative result into a
deployable single-pass scorer. Code at \texttt{github.com/yarikama/PAPIT}.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{ViT-side token pruning} for image classification is studied in
DynamicViT~\cite{rao2021dynamicvit} and AdaViT~\cite{meng2022adavit},
which insert a learned scorer in front of each ViT block. Token
merging (ToMe)~\cite{bolya2023tome} replaces scoring with similarity-aware
merging. Both lines of work assume access to ground-truth supervision
for training the scorer.

\textbf{LLM-side pruning for VLMs.}
FastV~\cite{chen2024fastv} prunes visual tokens \emph{inside} the LLM by
reading layer-$2$ attention and dropping low-attention tokens.
SparseVLM~\cite{zhang2024sparsevlm} extends this with hybrid
relevance/diversity strategies. Both require a forward pass through the
LLM before pruning.

\textbf{Dense CLIP signals.}
MaskCLIP~\cite{zhou2022maskclip} shows that with a small architectural
fix, patch-level CLIP features can be used for dense labels. Recent
work~\cite{darcet2024registers} also documents that ViTs allocate
register-like roles to a subset of patch tokens, which complicates
naive top-$k$ selection.

PAPIT differs from FastV in that pruning happens \emph{before} the LLM
sees the image, using a query-conditioned scoring step. The negative
result we report (\S\ref{sec:diagnostic}) shows why a naive instantiation
of this idea fails; the distillation we propose (\S\ref{sec:distill})
is the smallest viable fix.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{PAPIT pipeline}
\label{sec:method-pipeline}

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the three-stage pipeline. The image is
encoded by LLaVA's frozen vision tower (a CLIP ViT-L/14, no second
encoder is loaded) producing $576$ patch features $H{\in}\mathbb{R}^{576\times1024}$.
The prompt is encoded by the matching CLIP text encoder into a single
embedding $T{\in}\mathbb{R}^{768}$. A scoring step (\S\ref{sec:method-grad}
or \S\ref{sec:method-distill}) returns a vector $s{\in}\mathbb{R}^{576}$;
the top-$k$ indices select $k$ patches that, together with one
\emph{anchor} token (the mean of all $576$ patches, preserving global
context), are passed through LLaVA's MLP projector and into the LLM.
Position IDs are remapped to a dense $0,\dots,k$ sequence to keep the
LLM's positional encoding well-conditioned.

\subsection{Scorer 1: GradCAM on LLaVA's vision tower}
\label{sec:method-grad}

CLIP's contrastive objective only aligns the CLS token with text, so
naive patch--text cosine similarity is unstable across retention ratios.
We instead compute a Grad-CAM~\cite{selvaraju2017gradcam} attribution.
Let $h^{(L-1)}_i\in\mathbb{R}^{D}$ be the activation of patch $i$ at the
penultimate ViT encoder layer, and $\text{sim}(\text{CLS},T)$ the cosine
similarity between the projected CLS image embedding and the CLIP text
embedding $T$. We attribute $\text{sim}$ to each patch via
\begin{equation}
  s^{\text{gc}}_i \;=\; \mathrm{ReLU}\!\left(
        \sum_{d}\, \frac{\partial\,\text{sim}(\text{CLS},T)}{\partial\, h^{(L-1)}_{i,d}}
                   \,\cdot\, h^{(L-1)}_{i,d}
        \right).
  \label{eq:gradcam}
\end{equation}
Top-$k$ on $s^{\text{gc}}$ gives the retained set
$V_{\text{pruned}}=\{h_i\mid s^{\text{gc}}_i\in\mathrm{TopK}(s^{\text{gc}},k)\}$.

\subsection{Scorer 2: PAPIT-Distill}
\label{sec:method-distill}

Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic} will show that $s^{\text{gc}}$ is essentially
uncorrelated with the LLM's own attention to image tokens. A
multi-layer two-pass oracle (Table~\ref{tab:oracle}) confirms that
the missing signal lives \emph{inside} the LLM, with mid-layer
attention ($L\!\in\![8,24]$) consistently beating random.
\textbf{PAPIT-Distill} is a small predictor trained to mimic that
signal at single-pass cost.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
A $1.3$M-parameter $\mathrm{MLP}_4$. Each patch feature
$h_i\in\mathbb{R}^{1024}$ and the broadcast text embedding
$T\in\mathbb{R}^{768}$ are first projected to a hidden width
$d{=}384$, concatenated, and passed through three residual
LayerNorm--Linear--GELU blocks $f_1,f_2,f_3$ followed by a linear
scalar head $w$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  z_i^{(0)} &= [\,W_p h_i \,\Vert\, W_t T\,], \\
  z_i^{(\ell)} &= z_i^{(\ell-1)} + f_\ell\bigl(z_i^{(\ell-1)}\bigr), \quad \ell=1,2,3, \\
  s^{\text{d}}_i &= w^\top z_i^{(3)}.
\end{aligned}
  \label{eq:distill}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Supervision.}
We do a single LLaVA forward pass per training pair, take the layer-$8$
attention $A^{(8)}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$, sum over non-image
query positions to image keys, head-average, and renormalise to a
distribution $y\in\Delta^{575}$. We train with KL divergence,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L} \;=\; \mathrm{KL}\!\bigl(y\,\Vert\,\mathrm{softmax}(s^{\text{d}})\bigr),
  \label{eq:kl}
\end{equation}
on a $100{,}000$-sample mix of $50$K GQA, $30$K VQA~v2, and $20$K
TextVQA training pairs. Cached features take $\approx118$\,GB at fp16
and the predictor itself trains in $\approx30$\,s on a single A10G GPU.

\paragraph{Why supervise on layer~$8$.}
Section~\ref{sec:diagnostic} sweeps L\,${\in}\{2,8,16,24,31\}$ in a
two-pass oracle and finds mid-layer attention informative. As a
distillation target, we picked the layer whose distilled predictor
gives the best downstream accuracy averaged across the three
benchmarks at $k\!\in\!\{0.25, 0.5, 0.75\}$ ($N{=}100$ each); the
ranking is L\,${=}8$ ($+0.030$ over random) > L\,${=}16$ ($+0.024$) >
L\,${=}24$ ($+0.021$) > attention rollout ($-0.054$, actively worse
than random — the rollout target is hardest to predict and the
distilled signal is correspondingly noisy).

\subsection{Efficiency}
Self-attention cost is quadratic in sequence length. With $L_{\text{text}}\!\approx\!50$
text tokens,
\begin{equation}
  \text{FLOPs}_{\text{rel}}
  \;=\;\frac{(k+L_{\text{text}})^2}{(n+L_{\text{text}})^2},
  \label{eq:flops}
\end{equation}
giving a $10\times$ attention-FLOP reduction at $k{=}25\%$ ($n{=}576$).
\textbf{Wall-clock scoring latency} on g5.2xlarge (A10G, 20 samples,
mean $\pm$\,std, retention $0.5$): random $0.07{\pm}0.00$\,ms,
PAPIT-Distill $\mathbf{0.43{\pm}0.00}$\,ms, PAPIT-CLIP
(GradCAM) $65.23{\pm}6.08$\,ms, two-pass Oracle L=16
$216.17{\pm}1.17$\,ms. PAPIT-Distill is therefore
$\sim\!150\times$ faster than PAPIT-CLIP and $\sim\!500\times$ faster
than the oracle, while delivering an accuracy gain comparable to the
oracle (Table~\ref{tab:accuracy}).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\noindent\textbf{Models.}
LLaVA-1.5-7B (\texttt{llava-hf/llava-1.5-7b-hf}) at fp16, with its
native CLIP ViT-L/14 vision tower (\texttt{openai/clip-vit-large-patch14})
re-used for both LLM input and PAPIT scoring. PAPIT-Distill predictor:
$1.3$M-parameter $\mathrm{MLP}_4$, trained on cached features from
$100$K mixed-domain pairs.

\noindent\textbf{Datasets.}
GQA~\cite{hudson2019gqa} test-dev balanced, VQA~v2~\cite{goyal2017vqav2}
val-balanced, and TextVQA~\cite{singh2019textvqa} val. Each held-out
split uses $100$ samples per (dataset, retention) cell for downstream
accuracy and $100$ samples for diagnostics; binomial standard error
at $N{=}100$ is $\approx 0.05$. Accuracy: exact match for GQA, VQA
soft accuracy $\min(\mathrm{count}/3,1)$~\cite{goyal2017vqav2} for the
others.

\noindent\textbf{Baselines.}
\textbf{Unpruned} keeps all $576$ tokens. \textbf{Random} samples $k$
patches uniformly; under our pipeline this is an unusually strong
baseline because it preserves spatial coverage. \textbf{Oracle L=16} is
a two-pass upper bound: run LLaVA once on the full prompt, score by
layer-$16$ attention, prune, re-generate.

\subsection{PAPIT-CLIP matches but does not exceed random}
\label{sec:results-main}

Table~\ref{tab:accuracy} summarises the headline accuracy. Across all
three benchmarks and three retention ratios, the GradCAM-based
PAPIT-CLIP scorer is statistically indistinguishable from --- and
on TextVQA actively worse than --- random pruning: every (dataset,
retention) cell falls within $\pm 1$ binomial SE of Random
($\sigma\!\approx\!0.05$ at $N{=}100$). Figure~\ref{fig:qualitative}
shows that PAPIT-CLIP \emph{does} produce visibly localised
selections, yet this does not translate to a measurable downstream
gain.

\subsection{Why CLIP saliency is uninformative}
\label{sec:diagnostic}

Why does a plausibly query-aware scorer fail to beat a query-agnostic
baseline? We answer with a direct alignment diagnostic
(Table~\ref{tab:diagnostic}).
For each (image, prompt) we compute $s^{\text{gc}}\in\mathbb{R}^{576}$
and, separately, the average attention paid by the first $8$ generated
answer tokens to each of the $576$ image-token positions, taken from
LLaVA's last LLM layer averaged over heads, $s^{\text{llm}}\in\mathbb{R}^{576}$.
We report per-image Spearman rank correlation $\rho(s^{\text{gc}},s^{\text{llm}})$
and the Jaccard overlap of their respective top-$10\%$ patch sets.
A uniform-random selector yields top-$10\%$ Jaccard ${\approx}0.053$
in expectation.

\subsection{PAPIT-Distill: making the oracle deployable}
\label{sec:distill}

The oracle costs an extra full LLaVA forward, defeating the efficiency
goal. We instead distil it. \textbf{Architecture ablation}
(Table~\ref{tab:arch}) on the $5$K Scope-A cache picks $\mathrm{MLP}_4$
as the lightest predictor that beats both $\mathrm{MLP}_2$ and a
cross-attention block. \textbf{Supervision target ablation} on a
$20$K subset of the $100$K cache (val-KL on a $10\%$ held-out split,
$\mathrm{MLP}_4$, target~$\in\!\{A^{(8)},A^{(16)},A^{(24)},
\text{rollout}\}$) finds $L{=}8$ minimises val-KL ($0.094$) and
maximises top-$10\%$ Jaccard; rollout is hardest to predict because
it aggregates across all $32$ layers~\cite{abnar2020rollout}.
Critically, downstream accuracy follows distillability rather than
oracle strength: although Table~\ref{tab:oracle} shows $L{=}16$ is
the strongest oracle, the $L{=}8$ \emph{predictor} wins the
downstream ablation across the three benchmarks at
$N{=}100$ ($+0.030$ over random, vs.\ $+0.024$ for $L{=}16$,
$+0.021$ for $L{=}24$, and $-0.054$ for rollout — i.e.\ the rollout
distill is \emph{worse} than random, confirming that a hard-to-learn
target produces a useless predictor regardless of its oracle
quality). The deployed predictor is therefore $\mathrm{MLP}_4$
trained against $A^{(8)}$.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why doesn't PAPIT-CLIP collapse below random?}
LLaVA-1.5 exhibits substantial token redundancy~\cite{chen2024fastv},
so at $k\!\geq\!50\%$ the dominant accuracy driver is simply the
\emph{number} of retained tokens, not their identity. PAPIT-CLIP and
Random therefore sit within $\pm 1$ of each other on every cell.
Layer~$2$ attention fails for a related reason: in our two-pass setup
the prompt forward has not yet generated any answer token, so its
attention is dominated by positional and register-like
patterns~\cite{darcet2024registers}. Layers $8$--$24$ have integrated
the question with the image; layer~$31$ has already committed to
generation rather than visual reading.

\paragraph{Limitations and outlook.}
Both diagnostics and downstream accuracy are reported at $N{=}100$
per (dataset, retention) cell; binomial $\sigma\!\approx\!0.05$, so
single-cell gaps under $\pm 0.10$ are at most $\sim 2\sigma$. The
TextVQA $k\!=\!25\%$ gain ($+12.3$ over random) is therefore
$\sim 2.5\sigma$ — large enough to take seriously but worth
replicating at $N{=}500$+ before publication. Extending to LLaVA-1.6,
Qwen2.5-VL, and single-pass FastV-style integration of PAPIT-Distill
\emph{inside} the LLM are natural next steps. The headline finding ---
a $1.3$M-param MLP suffices to bridge a training-free, CLIP-derived
scorer to one that respects what the LLM actually attends to --- is
the smallest viable fix to a clean negative result, and is the
contribution we hope generalises beyond LLaVA-1.5.
